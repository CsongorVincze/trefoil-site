### Coulomb force

Conducting another experiment called the coulomb experiment we can observe that the force between electrically charged objects is proportional to the charges on the first object and also proportional to the charges on the second object. Besides that it is also inversely proportional to the distance squared. so we can come up with this proportionality:

$$
F \propto \frac{Q_1 Q_2}{r^2}
$$

We can turn this proportionality onto an equation if we put a constant before the right hand side.:

$$
F = k \frac{Q_1 Q_2}{r^2}
$$

Now we have two options. We define $k$ to be one (so it vanishes), and than we conclude the unit of charge. Or we define the unit of charge and conclude $k$. Usually we do the second option. We say that the unit of the charge will be Coulomb (C) (named after Coulomb) and we define it with the smallest charge (least in classical circumstances), the charge of the electron (or the proton). The charge of one electron is defined by $1,602\dots * 10^-19C$
So we choose to define charge and conclude what $k$ should be. Usually (for some reason) we don't use $k$, but we use $ \frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_0}$. It will be more comfortable for us to calculate with this expression in some problems. So, Coulomb law is the following:

$$
F = \frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_0} \frac{Q_1 Q_2}{r^2}
$$

### Electrostatic flux

👨‍🏫: Let's talk about electric flux! If you remember we discussed electric field lines. In summary; If we place a little test charge into the electric field. Then we ask the question.: In which direction would the test charge begin to move? then the tangent of the field lines (at the position we're interested in) would show us that specific direction. This is the first property of the field lines. The second is that the density of the field lines show us the strength of the electric field. So, putted in simple terms. where the lines are crowded we sense a strong electic field, where they are spares the field is weaker there. So, they are relly useful indicators. However its worth mentioning that electric field lines are just a handy tool for visualization, the elecric field not only exists on the lines.

First if you want the intuitive picture about flux it is something that kind of measures the number of electric field lines passing trough a surface.

🙋‍♂️: This is really nice, but in many cases the electris field is not homogenious and our surface isn't a square. What can we do then?

👨‍🏫: This is something that we do really often in physics. We might can not calculate the flux for a complex surface or a complex electric field. However what we can do is that we partition our surface into tiny regions where we can say that the surface isn't curved and the electric feild is homogenious -- with good approximation. Then we calculate the flux for all small surface elements and we sum them. So we'll end up with a formula that looks something like this.:

$$
\Phi = \sum_{i=0}^n \Delta \Phi_i = \sum_{i=0}^n \mathbf{E}_i \Delta \mathbf{A}_i
$$

So, here we partitioned our original surface into $n$ little surfaces. $\Delta \mathbf{A}_i$ denotes the $i$-th little surface and $\mathbf{E}_i$ is the electric field (as a vector) at this surface.
